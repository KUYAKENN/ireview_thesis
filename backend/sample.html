Prepositions: A Comprehensive Review

A preposition is a word or group of words that shows the relationship between a noun or pronoun and another word in the sentence. They often indicate location, time, or direction.

Examples: in, on, at, above, below, beside, between, among, to, from, with, by, for, about, of, etc.

Prepositions of Time

Preposition Usage Example
in Years, months, seasons, parts of the day in 1985, in the evening
at Specific times, noon, night, midnight at 2:30, at night
on Days of the week, dates on Friday, on April 21st
since From a point in the past to the present since 2004
for Duration of time for two years
by Due date, at the latest by the end of the week

Prepositions of Place

Preposition Usage Example
in Enclosed space in the car, in the room
at Specific point at the corner, at the bus stop
on Surface on the table, on the wall
to Movement towards to the store, to school
from Origin from the library, from home
towards Movement in a direction towards the door
across From one side to another across the street
through Movement inside something through the tunnel
between In the middle of two things between the trees
among Surrounded by many things among the flowers

Prepositions of Connection

Preposition Usage Example
of Possession, relationship the cover of the book
with Accompaniment, instrument with a friend, with a knife
from Origin, source from the beginning
in Inclusion, within in the list
on Basis, condition on the condition that
by Agent, means by train, by the author

Exercises

Exercise 1: Fill in the Blanks

Student: Excuse me, when are your office hours?

Instructor: I am available to meet you Thursday at 13:30 and 15:30.

Student: I have class that time. Can we meet at lunch?

Instructor: Is it urgent?

Student: Yes, I have some questions to ask you about the mid-term exam.

Instructor: You can come to my office at 11:00. Does that work for you?

Student: Yes, thank you. Can you remind me where your office is?

Instructor: My office is on the third floor, room A321 in the David Turpin Building.

Student: Thank you very much! See you then.

Exercise 2: Add Prepositions

Student: Hi Pr. Smith. I am Amanda Liu from your Introduction to Postmodernist Literature Class 204. I missed class for the past two weeks. I had a family emergency that I needed to attend to. I wonder if you might be able to give me notes on the lectures that I missed.

Instructor: I am sorry to hear that you had a family emergency to deal with. Unfortunately, I cannot give you my notes for fairness to other students. Do you have any friends who could share their notes with you?

Student: Yes, but they are a total mess. What did you discuss in class during my absence?

Instructor: Over the last two weeks, we looked at narrative techniques such as fragmentation and temporal distortion, with a specific emphasis on the work of David Foster Wallace. You will find information about these techniques in the textbook on page 208 and page 252.

Student: Thank you, I will review these pages.

Exercise 3: Correct the Mistakes

After reading excerpts of Wallace's Infinite Jest, there was something that seemed odd to me. In my notes, I wrote that the heavy use of endnotes is meant to disrupt the linear narrative. Given Wallace’s perception of reality as contingent, I can understand the latter interpretation. I’m having difficulty understanding how the conclusion can be said to be open-ended, though. I understand that it has something to do with the story eschewing chronological plot development and straightforward resolution, which support a wide range of readings, but the reliance on endnotes does seem to maintain some sense of narrative cohesion. I’ve talked to a few others in class, and none of them have been able to provide me with much insight. Would you be able to explain this to me? If you do not have time to do so via email, would you be willing to discuss it at the beginning of next class?
